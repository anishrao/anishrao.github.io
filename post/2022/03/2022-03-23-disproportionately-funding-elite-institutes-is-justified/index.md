If you are anything like me, reading the title of the post might have raised your eyebrows in disbelief.

So imagine, reading an email, where, I am asked to participate in a debate and defend this motion. _Defend a motion that I do not believe in._ This was my 2 mintutes of feeling like a lawyer who has to defend someone guilty, for the sake of fair representation in front of the law.

But even before we come to the point of me participating in this debate, comes the issue of me not being very comfortable on stage, and especially airing my views for everyone to hear. Imagine makeing this _worse_ by asking me to air views on something that I do not believe in.

Like everything in life, when it rains it pours. In the present case, if being asked to participate in a debate was not bad enough for me, the team that we were going up against had two **rockstar faculties** that I (along with most of the students at IISER Pune) adored. So, a difficult to defend motion, my own issues with stage fright, and a super popular and super smart team as opponents. What a non-anxiety inducing task right? And you know how this task ended up in my email? A friend of mine had suggested my name to defend this motion. I seriously need to choose some good friends. So, hit me up if you can do better 😅

I guess if you have read my previous post, you may know the drill by now. I get a task that I am not comfortable with, I panic. My by default response is say NO, and think of running away. Then some friends jump in, convince me to do tasks that are way out of my comfort zone, and I give in to this pressure. I have many instances of peer pressure being a helpful thing. So, unsurprizingly, the drill happened this time also, and I agreed to try and defend **_why favouring a few elite institutes is an okay thing to do_** - still an evil thing to defend in my mind. 😈

I did reflect on this development, and realized that defending this motion was a great example of how scientific inquiry works. The key principle of which is to _try your best to **disprove** your theory_. Defending the motion, therefore, was a great way to test and solidify my own views on how public funding of institutions should work. With this prelude, I thought of sharing here, exactly what I spoke when I was given the chance to air my initial defence of the motion in 2 minutes. Also, please read on till the last, to know how the debate ended.

> Today I stand before you all to defend the motion – that it is justified for a nation to disproportionately allocate its funds to its institutes of higher education.
> 
> I will essentially show that there is **no good reason to believe that the idea of disproportionate funding does not work**. My main arguments are that it emerges naturally, and more importantly, **it works!**
> 
> So, let’s see its natural emergence. Any institute that can award a degree, diploma or a certificate at the end of a course is a higher education institute. These institutes cater to a wide variety of fields like law, medicine, education, business, arts, music, science, etc. And even in a single discipline, like say sciences, they have specialized interests like institutes dedicated to biology, astrophysics, chemistry, etc. which goes on to show the diversity in the composition of higher education institutes. Another level of difference comes from the fact that some of these institutes are interested in doing high end research, some are interested in teaching while others are interested in doing both.
> 
> From this, we can see that there is an inherent disproportionality in the aspirations, and hence the needs of these higher education institutes. Because of which, a logical and natural outcome is disproportionate amounts of funding.
> 
> Sure, the other side can come and argue that “_oh look the sad state of institutes in that XYZ country, and see they fund their institutes disproportionately”_ to make a case for why this way of funding is bad. But, I would argue, that it shows that mindful design of policy, and its proper implementation is crucial for deciding the funding of institutes. This **does not** prove that disproportionate funding is flawed.
> 
> In order to make the idea of mindful implementations more concrete, we can look at successful ways of utilizing your limited financial resources.
> 
> We can look at a capitalist country like USA, which is a popular destination for higher education. One important disclaimer here is that they also rely sufficiently on student fees and private sector funding. But despite this, public sector funding to the institutions is disproportionate, where 17 out of the 4000 universities get ~40% of the government funding.
> 
> If the idea of following the steps of a capitalist state does not appeal to you, and you are more communist at heart, and believe in a society without social classes, we can look at China. Which has established programs like Project 211, with the aim to raise the standards of some of its universities to meet international standards. They roughly fund 6% of its institutes higher than the others.
> 
> We can also look at countries like Finland or Netherlands where the government decides the core funding to an institute using negotiations, funding formulas, performances, etc. This also happens in a disproportionate fashion where, say in the Netherlands, Erasmus university, despite having higher student enrolment, gets ~20% lower funds than say University of Twente.
> 
> From all these cases, we can see that most of the successful models of funding are disproportionate. Some of the countries have programs that are aimed to improve the quality of a small number of its institutes, while others have funding formulas where some institutes naturally get higher funds than others.
> 
> Now, if the other side wants to win this house, they need to point out that why disproportionate funding, **in general,** is an incompetent way of spending your limited financial resources. In other words they **need to show that there is no need of disproportionate funding**. Let the other side not fool you by showing effects improper policies as failures of disproportionate funding, and I believe that it will be irrational on our part to scrap this idea away.

Did you find this line of argument convincing? If not convincing, did you at least find this line of argument intellecually reasonable? My hope was to make people go past their initial knee-jerk reactions, and give this tipic a fair inquiry.

Regarding how the debate ended, **We lost.** 😅

After everyone got the chance to speak their intital prepared points in round 1, there was a second round, so as to respond to the points made by the opposing team. During my preparation for the debate, I had tried to think of the points that my opposition can make, and prepare a rough rebuttal for them. To my great delight, they did make points similar to ones that I had thought, which means that technically I had a rebuttal ready for them. Since my initial round went well, I think I got a bit cocky. Before the start of the second round, instead of preparing the best delivery of rebuttal, I asked to go first without much preparation. Unsurprizingly, I bombed on stage. I am glad that there is no recording of the event. 😁 I would not want to see myself on that stage during the second round.

So, that happened, and to make matters worse, the audience was on our side initially.

How does that make things worse?

Let me explain.

The format of our debate decides the winners by monitoring which side swayed most people. They do an initial voting to see where the audience stands at the beginning of the debate, ask the teams to make their points, then take the vote again. The side that swayed the most voters, wins. So, the side which has the audience with them as a result of knee-jerk response, has odds stacked against them. Since, the opposing team has to show reasons that their knee-jerk response is not right, or sow some amount of rightful and rational doubt. But this method, in my opinion, is still one of the better ways of judging a debate.

_**P.S.-** 1. I would have loved to make the following point during the question and answer round of the debate. But I was so embarassed and uncomfortable after my abysmal performance, that I just wanted to run away._

_One of the ‘cool’ professors form the opposing team made a remark that was very popular with everyone (judging by the round of applause that it recieved). No I am not jealous, you are jealous. 😁_

_He attributed the improvement in Indian cricket to breaking of the elite structure. To be honest, I was swayed by the argument as well (initially). But if you think about it, comparing cricket and higher education is not a fair comparision. Cricket is homogeneous, just one sport but higher education is inherenlty heterogeneous (as I point out in my initial arguments). A fair comparision would be comparing sports with higher education (both being heterogeneous entities). And in Indian scenario, we know that cricket gets hugely disproportionate funding than other sports and hence is doing way better. So while you think about this topic or similar topics, one thing to remember is that analogies and comparisions are great thinking tools, but one must make fair comparisions and do our best to avoid confirmation bias._
