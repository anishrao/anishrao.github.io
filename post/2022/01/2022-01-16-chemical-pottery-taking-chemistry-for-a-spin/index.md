Sometimes, it is easier, and convenient to avoid human effort for performing reactions, and automating the whole production process. I hope it is not just me who thinks of big and fancy robots performing complex actions on unmanned assembly lines, while thinking of automated reactions. How strange would it be to imagine a spinning machine for performing automated reactions?

Conventionally, spinning chemicals is a common way of separating entities with different densities, and forms the basis of a centrifuge. Similarly, a spin-coater spins a dispersion droplet thin to make uniform coatings, free of coffee-ring like stains. Such spinning devices like a centrifuge, spin-coater, stirring plates, etc. have rightfully earned their place in a chemical lab for performing separation, coating, and mixing experiments. But there is a new spinner in town. Writing in Nautre, the team of Bartosz Grzybowski introduce a new wall-less reactor that can spin complex multistep reactions, purifications and separation into action. These reactors are an alternate way of performing small to medium scale chemistries and can potentially be the shiny new tool in the chemist´s toolbox to create complex automated reactors without the need of complicated engineering.

## How does it work?

It is well known that liquids having varying densities can be vertically stacked against each other, and offer a unique possibility to perform sequential reactions, separations etc. Despite the simplicity and attractiveness of such systems, mechanical and capillary disturbances render these stacks inherently fragile, thereby limiting their practical utility. The team, however, started spinning the fragile vertical stacks of liquids, ultimately transforming them into robust horizontal ones, where, the centrifugal forces threw the denser fluid farther away, making it occupy the outermost ring.  Contrast this arrangement with what one would have obtained with a shotput player, who could easily throw lighter balls farther away than heavier balls. Heavier things fall farther if you use their own density for throwing them. In their experiments, depending on the choice of fluids, as well as the order of addition, the authors could routinely realize around 10-20 stacks of “wall-less” liquid reactors. Such horizontal stacks of liquids could then be utilized for a wide variety of chemistries. The astonishing feature of the work is the ease of designing and autonomously performing ingenious, and oftentimes complicated processes like multistep reactions, or complex purifications.

For a chemical reaction to happen, the reactants need to mix, and ultimately bump into each other in accurate orientations giving rise to the product. The rotating liquid stacks could either retain sluggish diffusive mixing (at constant rotation speeds), or more efficient mixing due to shear forces under alternating rotation speeds. The reactor could perform a three step Wittig reaction without the need of a human to separate, purify, and ultimately set up the subsequent reactions. The differences in the solubilities of the product of interest were potent enough for its migration into the adjacent fluid layer, thereby autonomously starting the subsequent steps into action. The authors used a similar design with minor modification i.e. utilizing fluctuating rotation speeds to perform the multistep synthesis of analgesic and anti-amoebic drugs. The advantage of the presented design was not limited to the convenience in performing the routine batch or flow synthesis, but also in performing separations requiring elaborate engineering hurdles in conventional reactors. For instance, the authors could perform simultaneous acidic and basic extractions, as opposed to conventional step-wise extractions from an organic phase. This idea was then further developed to selectively and more importantly, autonomously, extract a compound of interest from a complex mixture of molecules. Contrast the complex tasks autonomously undertaken with the spinning wall-less reactor with that of a chemist performing each step in the complex multiple step synthesis and purification. Although the flexibility of the system is profound, the systems comes up with its own challenge of the need to figure out the solvent systems of choice, order of additions etc. While the presented technology is definitely the shiny new toy in the toolkit of autonomous reaction design, only time will  show whether chemists actually buy, and play with this toy.
