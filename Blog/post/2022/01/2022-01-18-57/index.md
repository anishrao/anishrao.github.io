I did a thing during my PhD.

I was asked by a few friends to speak for an event organized by our institute's Chemphillic club. Honestly speaking, I have been a happy resident of my comfort zone most of my life. It is very rare event for me to venture outside this zone. Because of this, my knee-jerk response was to obviously let this opportunity go.

A few of my good friends, thankfully, convinced me not to let this opportunity pass. And under some peer pressure, I agreed to give the talk. Peer pressure is not necessarily bad, you see. Sometimes, it can lead to good outcomes.

I am not saying that the talk that I gave was great, or it did something huge for humanity.

The only good outcome was that I got to do something that I would have ideally not done. I managed to put my personal thoughts out there in front of a room full of people.

After overcoming the first hurdle of leaving the zone, the next task was what to choose what to speak on. Being a part of scientific institute, I did not want to add to the high number of scientific talks that we were already having.

Coincidently, during the same time, I was thinking about the scientific method, truth, and arrogance. Let me give a small pitch for my talk, and then direct you to see what I managed to speak that day.

What if I told you that all the things that we know today, are a product of humanity being wrong? The truths of today stand on the shoulders of yesterday's disproven theories. Those disproven theories, and people believing in the theories of yesterday may appear stupid or even dumb to us today. The possession of correct answer, can possibly fuel a bit of arrogance, and hostility towards those who don't. Since I had been thinking about this topic for a long time, I chose to speak on the topic of 'scientific arrogance', and start a conversation.
