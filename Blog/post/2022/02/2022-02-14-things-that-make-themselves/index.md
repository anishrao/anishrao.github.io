Chemistry, _according to me_, is the science of understanding the material world, and using this understanding to make useful things. I, by no means have any authority on defining what ‘_chemistry_’ is, but as of now, let us agree that this is a good enough definition of what chemistry aims to do. So, in the spirit of understanding and making things, let us undertake a simple task. The task is to make a single grain of salt.

How hard could this task be?

One of the best ways to start any build is to have a proper understanding and idea of what we want to make. A single grain of salt, let us assume, is a cube roughly 150 microns in size, or is equal to the width of two human hair. This I believe gives us a good enough image of the size and scale of what we want to make. The second thing to focus on is what tools we have at our disposal for completing the build. Since I am feeling quite generous, I give you an extremely fast working robot. A robot that can pick and place around **one million** atoms to the position of ‘_your_’ choice within a second! With such a fast working robot, can you guess how long will it take to get a single grain of salt?

Even with this hard and fast robot assembler, it will take _roughly 1800 years to get a single grain of salt_! Don’t take my word for it, in the spirit of inquiry, you must check this out for yourself. Calculate the number of atoms that comprise a single grain of salt, and divide it by the speed of the robot assembler to get the time needed for completing the build. Making salt does not appear to be easy work anymore does it? This example shows how slow the common pick-and-place type way of making things can be in making something so small. The salt crystals that you have in your house, thankfully, are not made by such robot assemblers. Humans have learned a thing or two about things that come into existence on their own. We can produce not just a single grain of salt, but heaps of salt by ‘_crystallization_’. Simply speaking, we have learned to convince sodium and chloride ions to come close and sit next to each other in neat and orderly fashion within a matter of hours to days. The science of understanding and ultimately designing self-made things is ‘_self-assembly_’. Enjoy this shiny new tool in your makers toolbox.

Now that you know of another tool that can be at your disposal, you can rightfully ask what all things can this new tool do? Can it make a car or TV much faster than your conventional pick-and-place type robots?

The answer is MAYBE.

There is a key idea that is hidden behind this magical image of things coming into existence without any external aid. The hidden idea is- **the need to teach the building blocks where do they belong in the final structure.** Without this information, things can come together in random orientations, and sequences producing wasteful things, instead of the intended product. This coding is crucial for the successful utilization of self-assembly processes. Scientists are learning the key design principles governing this field, and what complex structures can be made using self-assembly processes. Although self-assembly is still far from reaching its true potential in creating things that we use in our day to day life, but in our not-so-distant future, we can hope to see it become a legitimate means of manufacturing.
